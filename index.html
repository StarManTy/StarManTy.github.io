Honors Thesis

Post L.E.O. Reusable Chemical Rocket Booster

Orbital Mechanics • Propulsion Analysis • Systems Engineering

This honors thesis investigates a reusable chemical rocket booster designed for post–Low Earth Orbit (LEO) missions, with an emphasis on payload delivery to interplanetary transfer orbits and rapid booster return to LEO. The work evaluates engine-based orbital maneuvers without reliance on aerobraking, focusing instead on propulsion-driven efficiency, reusability, and mission flexibility.

Technical Focus

  • Post-LEO mission architecture and reusable booster design
  • Engine-based orbital transfer maneuvers (LEO → transfer orbit → LEO return)
  • Aldrin cycler path considerations for future Mars missions
  • Trade studies comparing propulsion approaches for deep-space operations

Methodology

  • Analytical orbital mechanics calculations for transfer and return trajectories
  • Performance modeling using nine SpaceX Raptor 2 Vacuum engines
  • Mass budgeting based on Super Heavy–class booster scaling
  • Delta-v, thrust-to-weight, and propellant consumption analysis
  • Comparative review of chemical, nuclear thermal, and electric propulsion systems

Key Design Parameters

  • Propellant mass: ~3,400 metric tons
  • Estimated dry mass: ~250 metric tons
  • Total initial mass: ~3,650 metric tons (no payload)
  • Engine configuration: 9× Raptor 2 Vacuum
  • Thrust: ~258 tf per engine
  • Specific impulse: ~330 s

Tools & Skills

  • Orbital mechanics and trajectory analysis
  • Engineering trade studies and system modeling
  • LaTeX for technical documentation
  • Physics-based performance calculations
  • Research synthesis and technical writing

Outcome: The thesis demonstrates the feasibility of a reusable, propulsion-only post-LEO booster architecture capable of supporting future Mars transfer missions while maintaining rapid reusability and operational flexibility.
